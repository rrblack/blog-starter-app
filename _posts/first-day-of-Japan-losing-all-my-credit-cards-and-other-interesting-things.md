Well the time has finally come. I am actually in Japan and it still doesn’t feel real. Walking down Akihabara yesterday I kept saying to myself “this has to be a dream I’ll wake up from any minute”. Today is the first day of our orientation that be will starting in about 3 hours from now. I figured I’d take some spare time to write out some of my first impressions and thoughts from yesterday before I forget.

As most of you know the flight to Japan is 13 hours long. Surprisingly, however, it really was not too bad this time around. Maybe because I knew what to expect and how to pass the time, but it seemed more like a 6 hour trip than a 13 hour trip. Some of the hours flew by, probably in part thanks to me sleeping intermittently.

After arriving at the Haneda airport, we had to walk around what felt like the entire airport. In order to enter immigration we had to first obtain a blue piece of paper that essentially said we didn’t have corona. This was verified by uploading documents to an app we had to install before coming to Japan and showing the confirmation screen. To get this paper, we had to go a very very long roundabout way through many different long hallways just to get this paper. Then to make it even better we had to go all the way back to where we started to hand it to the immigration officer. Why we couldn’t just show the officer the app in the first place I have no answer for other than: Japan.

Once we got out of customs and immigration we were greeted by multiple people holding signs with JET written on them. They then got us to put tags on our bags, loaded them onto the bus for us then drove us to the hotel we are staying. The hotel is called the Keio Plaza Hotel and it is definitely a upper class hotel. Just to put into perspective the attention to detail the Japanese put into everything. This is what happened when I took a shower:

![Shower room in Japan](/assets/blog/first-day-in-japan/pxl_20220731_083036920.jpg)

They have designed it to where if you take a shower and it steams up the bathroom, the part of the mirror in front of the sink will never fog up. I thought this was really cool and have never seen anything like this before. Just goes to show you the amount of care and thought that goes into Japanese design philosophy.

After the shower I decided to take a walk around Tokyo and head to Akihabara, a place I frequently visited last time I was in Japan that is know for their electronics and games. However, before I got on the train I realized something: I had lost all of my credit cards. Apparently while I was loading my train card with money, I didn’t realize how thick my wallet had become from adding yen to do (yen bills are a lot larger and wider than dollar bills) and it pushed all the cards on the right side of my wallet out and onto the ground. I honestly wasn’t sure if I would ever find them so I started the process of freezing/ordering card replacements. I spent about 40 mins looking around and was on the phone with my bank trying to figure out how on earth I would get a US bank card send to a US address when I came back to the station and saw the kanji ”忘れた物” which means lost item, on a sign leading to a small building. This place was a lost and found.

I told to the lady on the phone to give me 2 minutes before she canceled the card while I talked to the people at the station. Thankfully thanks to what I already knew in Japanese, I was able to tell them exactly what I lost and what I was looking for. They went to the back for a second and to my surprise they brought back all of the cards that I had lost. I was honestly a bit in shock that not a single one was missing and that they were actually there. If I were to lose any cards in America I really do not think I would be able to find them again as easily as I did in Japan. I think this was a great demonstration of the honesty of the Japanese people and it made me love Japan even more.

After that I finally made it to Akihabara only to find how much it has changed because of corona. So many buildings that I visited before had either gone out of business, changed hands or didn’t have nearly the amount of selection that they once had. Akiba honestly felt like a shell of its former self and it was really sad to see. The lack of tourism has really destroyed many businesses in Japan. I hope once tourism returns we can see some of these abandoned buildings brought back to life and return the district to what it once was.

Last thing I want to talk about before I head off for the day is the humidity. I honestly was taken back a bit from how humid it was. I understood it being humid around 2,3 4 pm but I did not expect it to be so humid at 8, 9pm at night. Even in the dark of night, standing outside for even a minute will make you sweat so much its not even funny. This will definitely be something I’ll have to adjust to. Even though Tennessee is humid it at least is never humid once its night time.

That’s all for today’s blog; thanks for reading. I didn’t have the chance to take many photos yesterday since everything was so fast paced so sorry if a wall of text was sort of boring. I will be adding a lot more photos to the blog once I get settled. See you all next time.
