<h1> We are finally married and moved in</h1>

After an extremely busy January and February with getting both married and moving, I am happy to announce it is finally over.

<h2> Trouble at the city hall </h2>
Going to the city hall to register our marriage reminded me of how difficult it can be living as a foreigner in Japan. For what took the Japanese couple getting married next to us only 10 minutes, it took us over 5 hours of waiting. The issue was that the American government decided to no longer offer proof via an affidavit showing that the person in question has never been married before and can legally marry.

Up until September 2025 the embassy offered this service, but for some reason decided to cut it, causing our lives and many other Americans' much stress and difficulty. It's strange, but in Japan, each city hall seems to have their own unique way of dealing with marriage registration for foreigners. For our city hall, since the new changes in September there hadn't been a single case of an American and a Japanese citizen getting married. Japan loves precedent and previous examples, but there wasn't one, so we kind of had to make our own.

Thankfully the city hall was understanding and willing to work with us, but it wasn't without its difficulties. Since the American embassy doesn't offer affidavits anymore, that meant we had to find a way to get our own. I guess the American government was just hoping Japan would accept a verbal "Trust me bro," but if you know anything about Japanese culture, without an official seal, "Trust me bro" will never work for anything. They love the art of 確認 in Japan, which is to check, check, and triple-check everything before making a decision.

Long story short, we managed to find an online notary that could sign an affidavit for us. But even after having it signed, getting it approved and recognized by the city hall was the hardest part. Even three weeks later after we had submitted the paperwork, they were still in the process of getting it all checked and approved. This is just one of the difficulties of living in Japan as a foreigner that I wanted to talk about today.


<h2>Life, but everything is on hard mode </h2>

Imagine your current life in whatever country you are in and how used to everything you are. Everyone speaks the same language as you, you all share the same humor, jokes, and memes. While maybe not exciting, life is comfortable and predictable, right? Now imagine going to a country where the culture is the complete opposite of yours, no one speaks your language, you don't understand any of the country's humor or famous actors. Literally everything is foreign to you. You have to relearn everything from scratch. Almost as if being born again as an infant.

That was me when I first came to Japan. I didn't know any Japanese, anything about the culture, and almost nothing made sense.

<b>  <i> So what, are you trying to say how cool you are that you understand Japanese culture now? Ok we get it bro. </i> </b>

No, not at all. What I want to say is that even after 4 years of living here in Japan, learning the language, understanding the culture, and knowing who famous Japanese actors are and understanding Japanese jokes and humor, living in Japan can still be difficult as a foreigner.

While living in Japan is great in many aspects, there are still many difficulties that us foreigners face on a daily basis. For those who come after, I wanted to write down my impressions and record what I've experienced in my 4 years of living here so many others could relate or not feel alone when they come here.

<h1>1. The staring </h1>
Probably the very first thing I noticed when walking by myself in Japan, even back in 2019 when I first came, was the staring. And I'm not talking about being paranoid like "EvErYoNe's ALWays LooKing aT Me." I mean like you sit down on a train, bus, or whatever and you make brief eye contact with someone (usually some old grandpa or granny). Naturally you glance away after a second. But wait, what's this weird feeling? You glance again and they are still staring right at you. They never broke eye contact. "Surely it can't be," You glance again one more time. <b>They are still staring at you.</b> You decide to stand your ground this time and stare back, leading them to finally look away.

This sort of situation happens more times than I can count. After 4 years here, it's more like "yep, that's right, I'm a foreigner in your country, what are you gonna do about it? Sorry granny, but I pay my fair share of taxes here too."

My hypothesis for why people stare so often is pretty simple: they don't see foreigners very often. But wait, I live in Tokyo where they see foreigners every day. Maybe they think I'm attractive? There's no telling why people stare at me, but at this point there's no use paying any mind to it. It does get tiring though, and makes you feel like there's something wrong with you just for existing.

<h1>2. Only looking at your Japanese partner during conversation </h1>
Being the complete opposite of the first one, another common thing that happens here as a foreigner is having the person you are talking to not even glance at you a single time during the conversation, instead looking only at your Japanese friend or partner. This one gets to me a lot more than the first, because you can often be treated as if you don't even exist.

I know the reason is likely because they think I either can't understand Japanese, or they just assume my wife will explain everything to me later but please, at least glance at me once during the conversation to acknowledge my existence.

<h1>3. Complimented for saying even the most basic Japanese</h1>
I don't get this one as much anymore, and I think it's pretty much become a meme for most people coming to Japan. No matter what you say in Japanese, Japanese people will say "おおお、日本語上手ですね！！" (Your Japanese is sooo good). I remember coming to Japan the first time in 2019 and speaking literally 2 words of Japanese, "猫耳" (cat ears), and the person hit me with the 日本語上手. The most ironic part is that I didn't even understand what that phrase was or what they were trying to say.

If we were to reverse it, imagine someone coming to America and saying one word: "Hello." And then you say, "WOW YOUR ENGLISH IS SO GOOD. THAT IS AMAZING." It's so ridiculous it's almost condescending, right? That's how it feels for me as a foreigner every time someone says that to me. They would never say "oh, your Japanese is so good" to a fellow Japanese person, so it's just another subtle way to point out the fact that you aren't Japanese and never will be.

However, I know that for the Japanese person saying it, they mean well. They consider Japanese to be one of the hardest languages on earth to learn, and the fact that someone not born in the country can even speak a part of their language is truly amazing to them. But for us foreigners who have been living here for many years, it becomes less and less of a compliment and more like "oh, my Japanese is still bad enough that they feel the need to say something."

<h1>4. Housing discrimination </h1>
On things that would be blatantly illegal in America and likely other countries, we have landlords denying foreigners the ability to rent property simply based on their nationality, which would probably top the list.

Yes, you heard that right. Landlords are free to discriminate on who lives in their property at their discretion. That can mean they flat-out reject you if you aren't Japanese, or it can mean only allowing certain nationalities. For example, flat-out denying Indians, Indonesians, or Vietnamese, but allowing Americans.

Sounds racist? According to landlords here, it's simply "risk assessment." You see, a lot of landlords in Japan assume that foreigners don't know how to sort trash, are noisy at night, and would disturb other residents. So to avoid even the potential of that happening, it's easier to just flat-out deny them instead. Same for race selection, Indians make spicy food whose smell could stick to walls or waft over to neighbors, so we'll deny them but keep the Americans, etc.

I remember when I first was looking for an apartment, I had the real estate agent call multiple listings to check if they were available. I was listening to them talking on the phone, and 70% of the time, the conversation went something like this:

"Hi, is this property available?"

"Yes it is!"

"Great, there's just one thing I want to double check. The applicant is American, will that still be alright?"

"Oh, I'm sorry, this property is not available to foreigners."

"Ok, sorry, thank you for your time."

It was quite a shock when I first came to Japan, and likely to many other foreigners as well. Thankfully I now live in a house with my wonderful wife Yukina, so I will never have to deal with this again.

<h1>5. Being spoken to rudely, like I am a dog </h1>
In case you didn't know already, Japanese society is built on respect and honor, unless you are a foreigner, in which case apparently people can speak to you however they want. One of the biggest shocks since coming to Japan has been the way some people speak to me. There are many subtle ways to show displeasure or dislike for someone without explicitly saying so in Japanese. Having lived here for 4 years, talking daily in Japanese with my Japanese wife and having her teach me, I have learned to recognize when someone is being rude just by their subtle tone of voice and expression. This has happened both when I was alone and when she was with me.

One example: she and I went to ship a return at the local convenience store. We were just planning on printing the shipping label and packing it later, but we didn't realize that as soon as you print the shipping label, you are expected to pack it right then and there. When we got to the counter, this middle-aged balding man (one of the store managers btw) scanned the barcode and immediately had an attitude: "WHERE IS THE BOX?? Bring the box RIGHT NOW. Right this minute." Obviously in Japanese, but that was the tone and manner he was using. We told him we didn't have the box but could get it right away since my apartment was just down the road, and he replied, "YOU HAVE 5 MINUTES TO GET IT HERE OR I'M CANCELLING THE WHOLE ORDER."

It was obviously an empty threat, as 15 minutes later we came back with the box and he was gone. A kind foreigner working there helped us finish the shipping and get it sent off. Needless to say, his attitude and demeanor were completely uncalled for. Yes, I'm sorry I didn't know to bring the box the first time, but that doesn't give you the right to treat me like a stupid foreigner who understands nothing about Japan. I'm sure many Japanese people have made the same mistake, but I doubt he treated them the way he treated me.

Later that night Yukina wrote to the store to file a complaint. A day later, the store owner confirmed that they had checked the security footage and that his attitude was completely inappropriate and he was reprimanded. Here is the letter if you want to see:

Another time recently, Yukina answered the door for me to collect a package in my name. She told me the delivery man's first words were "Is this where the foreigner lives?". She said "No this is the Porter residence" and took the package (thank you, I love you Yukina). But can you imagine that sort of talk in any other country? Like America for example, you see a Japanese name on the package you deliver and instead asking "Is this the Tanaka residence?" you ask "is this where the Jap lives?". Do these people even understand how racist that comment comes across?

One more example involves a delivery man (why is it always packages?), where yet another grumpy old man was delivering my credit card. Obviously the name on the package was mine, so it was a foreign name he was delivering to. The moment he rang the doorbell, he already had an attitude; very similar to the convenience store guy I mentioned earlier. Normally in Japan you'd say something like "Hello, I am with so-and-so company and have a package for Porter Kyle." This guy's first words were, "COME OUTSIDE RIGHT NOW." Like, brother, I'll come to the door, but calm down. I replied in the most polite Japanese I could muster despite his rudeness. But to make matters worse, even after coming to the door, he refused to give me my credit card. The reason? Even though the name was exactly the same, it was written in Japanese rather than English, which he said didn't match my ID. The thing is, it wasn't even my fault; the site I applied on didn't allow English characters to begin with, so I was forced to use Japanese. Which leads me to my next point:

<h1>6. Online forms on Japanese websites not allowing English characters, middle names, or more than 9 letters for a full name </h1>

This is one of the most frustrating things about living in Japan. Since the population is 99% ethnic Japanese, it makes sense for everything to be catered to Japanese residents, but for the love of God, please consider foreign residents at least once. It goes without saying that most of Japanese IT is stuck in the 90s, but application pages take it to another level. A good portion of them only allow 9 characters max, with no English or spaces allowed. You can see how this might pose a problem. A lot of foreigners have an English first and middle name, which alone is already likely more than 9 letters and won't fit. Even if you don't add a space between your first and middle name, or try to write your name in Japanese (chances are it still won't fully fit), you will run into authentication errors where they check against your registered name at the city hall and say, "There's a space between your first and middle name but you didn't write that in the application, so we can't approve it." All while I am screaming at the website, "YOU DIDN'T LET ME WRITE IT IN THE FIRST PLACE."

The same thing happened with this credit card recently. They didn't allow any English letters at all, but the ID issued in Japan when you first enter the country and register at the city hall uses the name exactly as it appears in your passport, all in English. So when the time came to verify my ID, the rude old man said, "Nope, they don't match. Not gonna give you the card." Even though: 1. they are the exact same name, and 2. I was never given an option to input English. This has happened with at least 2 banks now, and even now I still haven't gotten one of them sorted. 

Same thing for taxes, every year there is ふるさと納税 where you can buy produce from rural areas in exchange for a tax discount. The only problem is the site I ordered from (the most widely used site for this, by the way) would not allow me to put a space between my first and middle name, causing a mismatch with my city hall information and preventing them from processing my tax exemption. Yukina is currently having to fill it out by hand and turn it in manually at city hall. She also had to call the credit card company and get them to change the name over the phone so they could resend a new card. She has always been there for me when these sorts of issues and injustices come up in Japan, and I am really grateful.

Despite being Japanese, even Yukina herself is occasionally subject to the same treatment as a foreigner simply because we are together. Ethnically she is Japanese but depending on her makeup she can look slightly non-Japanese due to her complexion and white skin. Due to this, she will sometimes also be talked to in English, handed an English menu, or talked down to like she doesn't understand Japanese culture by other Japanese. We are automatically assumed to just be foreign tourists. While it is frustrating to me who speaks Japanese, it is even more so frustrating for her as she is getting caught in the crossfire simply for being associated with me. She specifically asked me to include this part because it bothers her so much. Thankfully we have each other to support and both struggle through it together.

But if <i>me, with a Japanese wife,</i> is having this many issues, I can only imagine how much more difficult it is for the average foreigner who is completely on their own.

For a country that is economically struggling and facing a shrinking population, this inflexibility and refusal to modernize archaic systems is going to put them decades behind other modern countries sooner than later. While other countries are openly accepting talented foreign workers (yes, that includes America), Japan is actively trying to make life harder for foreigners and reduce the number coming here. Or if they do come here, "limit the foreigner to 5% of the population and borrow their labor while they're young... but when they get old, see to it that they return to their home country" as Sanseito party leader Kamiya Sohei said openly on live television on February 8th, 2026. The original article has since been deleted. I only found it through the Web Archive. Draw your own conclusions. The 400 plus comments also were in agreement with some saying "we don't even need 5% of them here" <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20260218175536/https://news.yahoo.co.jp/articles/f98da34d9350723b2c26da89f49752855c9f939a" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Source</a> I highly recommend translating and reading this if you want a good look at where Japan is headed.

Also recently in the news is a Japanese person who accused a foreigner of trying to rob them, claiming the foreigner shouted "Give me your money" in English. But when the police checked the nearby security footage, it turned out to be a completely made-up story and a lie. Why would a Japanese person randomly lie and accuse a foreigner of stealing when they did no such thing? And what do you think would have happened to that person if there wasn't video footage to refute the claim? Do you think the police would have taken the foreigner's side or the Japanese side? As a foreigner, this sort of situation is terrifying to me, especially when you consider Japan has a 99.9% conviction rate. Even if you didn't commit a crime, they can hold you indefinitely until they force a conviction out of you. There are many cases of these false convictions and I encourage you to look them up. You can read the article of the false accusation <a href="https://news.jp/i/1402574006431760399?c=768367547562557440" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">here.</a> Make sure to translate it.

All this leads me to my last point:

<h1>7. The political climate towards foreigners in general</h1>

To anyone who has been following Japanese news even slightly, I'm sure you might have heard of the Sanseito party or perhaps the "foreigner issue" in Japan. In Japanese they call it "外国人問題", implying foreigners are a problem that must be dealt with. The Sanseito party is a new right-wing political party in Japan that has been gaining a lot of traction and popularity in Japan thanks to being very vocal about the problems that foreigners are causing. So much so that it caused the current ruling party of Japan, the LDP, (which you might have seen on the news; the prime minister? yea that's her party) to even start cracking down. The LDP has been in power for decades and has been pretty neutral on foreign topics. 

Not much has changed since the LDP has been in power many many years ago. But a mini revolution started when this Sanseito party finally started offering change and dealing with long term issues the LDP had been ignoring. This ignited a fire in the LDP and they chose the Prime Minister Takaichi, who for a very long time has actually been getting stuff done within the LDP party; including dealing with foreigners.

Now, I actually agree with some of what the LDP is trying to do. However, the sentiment from parties like Sanseito and the overall messaging of "foreigners are a problem" is what I take issue with. By all means, please deport illegal immigrants and people not paying their fair share. Unfortunately, all foreigners are getting lumped into that messaging, including the ones doing everything right. So when rhetoric intended for illegal residents like "we don't need foreigners" gets out, it gets spun into something that is more or less openly xenophobic. 

Seeing this daily in the news, from politicians, and on TV has been pretty discouraging, obviously as a foreigner living here. It's a bit like a reverse ICE situation, except I'm not even illegal. People being against your existence simply because you aren't native Japanese will never not be sad. And it's for these reasons that I probably won't be changing my citizenship to Japanese. When I first came to Japan, I was eager and excited to eventually become a citizen because I loved Japan (and still do). But with them raising the residency requirement from 5 to 10 years, and with the reality that no matter how well I speak Japanese or understand the culture and customs, I will always be judged first by what I look like, it's quite disappointing and sad. 

<h1>Closing thoughts </h1>
I have spent 4 years of my life here, learned the language, been working brutal night shifts that no Japanese want to do, with a 3 hour round trip commute never missing a day, paid my taxes on time and in full every single time, learned and respected Japanese culture, even married a Japanese citizen and yet I am still treated like a second class citizen that is wasting up precious resources in Japan and needs to get sent back to where they came from. There is a part of me that wants to give the Japanese want they want and just do it. Just take Yukina with me to America and straight up leave everything behind in Japan. They didn't appreciate me or my labor when I was here, maybe they will have a second thought in 15 years. But by that time I won't be needing Japan, Japan will be needing me.

That's not even mentioning how the LDP has recently introduced legislation to increase the price of visa renewals by up to over 2,500%. Yes, you read that right. What used to be around $65 for permanent residency is now going up to around $1,600. The wages though? Those haven't changed. Same goes for the general visa as well. It will be close to $360 now. And a lot of people only get one year visas, meaning they'll have to pay that every single year just to simply live in Japan, all while wages stay the same. For the average person from India, Vietnam, or Indonesia it might make sense to immigrate to Japan, but with these changes they won't be able to afford to even live in Japan anyway; which is exactly why they are increasing the fees. However, for an American, the incentives to work in Japan are very little. Why work for one third of the salary I could make back home? Especially in a climate where you are not even wanted despite speaking the language and understanding the culture.

Now I'm sure some people reading are thinking "Wow it sounds like Japan is miserable for you, you should stop complaining and just go back home" or "You're the one who chose to come to Japan so you have no right to complain, it's their country after all." And you are completely right, it is their country. And they can be as racist and xenophobic as they want, but does that make it right? Does that mean I shouldn't have at least a say in how I feel I am being treated? Just as racism should not be tolerated and is unacceptable if a Japanese person immigrated to America, I feel it is equally wrong for racism to just be accepted as "it's Japanese culture get over it." I pay taxes and work just like Japanese people. I don't have the right to vote here, but surely my voice should be heard as much as everyone else's, shouldn't it? Even if I decide to eventually go back to America, I want to leave this blog for those who come after and try to start a new life in Japan. They will probably be like I was, with rose-tinted glasses and thinking they will want to become Japanese, but after a few years the reality will set in, and I want this blog to be there for them to read. To know that someone else went through the exact same feelings that they did.

I brought up these points today not to talk badly about Japan. I love Japan. I love(d)? living here, and 90% of the people I have interacted with have been wonderful. I mean, I literally met my wife here, this place is home to me. But it's <i>because</i> it's home to me that I feel the need to speak out about my experience living here and hopefully see things change for the better. I think a lot of Japanese people might not realize how the way they talk to or interact with foreigners can come across as discriminatory. I wanted to take the time to share this perspective so more people could understand what it's like for us foreigners, told by someone who actually speaks Japanese and lives here. (Not a tourist.)
