<h1> We are finally married and moved in</h1>

After an extremely busy January with getting both married and moving, I am happy to announce it is finally over. Well, maybe no quite. There are still a lot of moving boxes we have yet to sort through. But the hardest part is over now.

<h2> Trouble at the city hall </h2>
Going to the city hall to register our marriage reminded me of how difficult it can be living as a foreigner in Japan. For what took the Japanese couple getting married next to us only 10 minutes, took us over 5 hours of waiting. The issue lies in that the American government decided to no longer offer any proof via an affidavit showing that the person in question has never been married before and can legally marry. 

Up until September 2025 the embassy offered this service but for some reason decided to cut it, causing our lives and many other American's much stress and difficulty. It's strange but in Japan, each city hall seems to have their own unique way of dealing with marriage registration to foreigners. For our city hall, since the new changes in September there hadn't been a single case of an American and a Japanese citizen getting married. Japan loves precedent and previous examples but there wasn't one so we kind of had to make our own.

Thankfully the city hall was understanding and willing to work with us but it wasn't without its difficulty. Since the American embassy doesn't offer affidavits anymore that meant we had to find a way to get our own. I guess the American government was just hoping Japan would accept verbal word of "Trust me bro", but if you know anything about Japanese culture, without an official seal, "Trust me bro" will never work for anything. They love the art of "確認" in Japan, which is the check, check, and triple check everything before making a decision.

Long story short we managed to find an online notary that could sign an affidavit for us. But even after having it signed, getting it approved and recognized by the city hall was the hardest part. Even as I write this now, they still are in the process of getting it all checked and approved even though we submitted the papers literally over three weeks ago. This is just one of the difficulties living in Japan as a foreigner that I wanted to talk about today.


<h2>Life, but everything is on hard mode </h2>

Imagine your current life in whatever your country you are in and how used to everything you are. Everyone speaks the same language as you, you all share the same humor, jokes and memes. While maybe not exciting, life is comfortable and predictable right? Now imagine going to a country where the culture is the complete opposite as you, no one speaks your language, you don't understand any of the country's humor or famous actors; literally everything is foreign to you. You have to relearn everything from 0. Almost as if being born again as an infant.

<b>  <i> Alright Kyle, what is your point? </i> </b>

What I want to say is, that was literally me when I first came to Japan. I didn't know any Japanese, anything about the culture and almost nothing made sense. 

<b>  <i> So what, are you trying to say how cool you are that you understand Japanese culture now? Ok we get it bro. </i> </b>

No not at all. What I want to say is, that even after 4 years of living here in Japan, learning the language, understanding the culture, knowing who famous Japanese actors are and understand Japanese jokes and humor, living in Japan can still be difficult as a foreigner.

While living in Japan is great in many aspects, there are still many difficulties than us foreigners face on a daily basis. For those who come after, I wanted to write my impressions and record what I've experienced in my 4 years of living here so many others could relate or not feel alone when they come here. 

<h3>1. The staring </h3>
Probably the very first thing I noticed when walking by myself in Japan, even in 2019 when I first came, was the staring. And I'm not talking about being paranoid like "EvErYoNes ALWays loOking aT Me". I mean like you sit down on a train, bus, or whatever and you make brief eye contact with someone (usually some old granpa or granny). Naturally you glance away after a second. But wait, what's this weird feeling? You glance again and they are still staring right at you. They never broke eye contact. "Surely it can't b-" You glance again one more time. <b>They are still staring at you </b>. You decide to stand your ground this time and stare back, leading them to finally look away. 

This sort of situation happens more times than I can count. After 4 years here its more like "yep thats right I'm a foreigner in your country, what are you gonna do about it? Sorry granny, but I pay my fair share of taxes here too." 

My hypothesis for why people stare so often is pretty simple: they don't see foreigners very often. But wait, I live in Tokyo where they see foreigners everyday. Maybe they think I'm attractive? There's no telling why people stare at me everyday but at this point theres no point paying any mind to it. It does get tiring sometimes though and makes you feel like there's something wrong with you just for existing.

<h3>2. Only looking at your Japanese partner during conversation </h3>
Being completely opposite of the first one, another common thing that happens here as a foreigner is having the person you are talking to not even look at you a single time during the conversation and instead look at your Japanese friend/partner etc. This one gets to me a lot more than the first one because you can often be treated as if you don't even exist.

I know the reason is likely because they think I either can't understand Japanese or just assume my wife will explain everything to me later, but please at least glance at me once during the conversation to acknowledge my existence. 

<h3>3. Complemented at saying even the most basic Japanese</h3>
I don't get this one as much now and I think it's pretty much become a meme for most people coming to Japan where no matter what you say in Japanese, Japanese people will say "おおお、日本語上手ですね！！" (Your Japanese is sooo good). Like I remember coming to Japan the first time in 2019 and speaking literally 2 words of Japanese "猫耳" (cat ears), and the person hit me with the 日本語上手。But the most ironic part is that I literally didn't even understand what that word was or what they were trying to say.

Like if we were to reverse it, imagine someone coming to America etc and saying one word. "Hello". And then you say "WOW YOUR ENGLISH IS SO GOOD. THAT IS AMAZING" It's so ridiculous its  almost condescending right? That's how it feels for me as a foreigner every time someone says that to me. They would never say "oh your japanese is so good" to another fellow Japanese, so it's just another subtle way to point out the fact you aren't Japanese and never will be.

However, I know that for the Japanese person saying it, they mean well as they consider Japanese like the hardest language on earth to learn and the face someone not born in the country can even speak a part of their language, is truly amazing to them. But for us foreigners living here for many years, it becomes less and less of a complement and more like "oh my Japanese is still bad enough that they have to feel the need to compliment me". 

<h3>4. Housing discrimination </h3>
On things that would be blatantly illegal in America and likely other countries, we have denying foreigners the ability to rent property simply based off of their nationality, which would probably be number 1. 

Yes you heard that right. Landlords are free to discriminate on who lives in their property at their discretion. That can mean they flat out reject you if you aren't Japanese or it can mean selecting and allowing only a certain nationality. For example flat out denying Indians, Indonesians, Vietnamese etc but allowing Americans. 

Sounds racist? No not at all, it is simply risk assessment in Japan. You see, alot of landlords in Japan assume that foreigners don't know how to sort trash, are noisy at night and that would disturb other residents. So to avoid even the potential of that happening, it's easier to just flat out deny them instead. Same for race selection. Indians make spicy food which the smell could stick to walls, or waft over to other neighbors so we will deny them but keep the Americans etc.

I remember when I first was looking for apartment I had the real estate agent call multiple listings to check is they were available. I was listening to them talking on the phone and 70% of the time, the conversation went something like this:

"Hi is this property is available?" 

"Yes it is!" 

"Great, there is just one thing I want to double check. It is an American, will that still be alright?" 

"Oh I'm sorry this property is not available to foreigners" 

"Ok sorry, thank you for your time."

It was quite a shock when I first came to Japan and likely to many other foreigners as well. Thankfully I now live in house with my wonderful wife Yukina so I will never have to deal with this again. 

As I get to my last point, as much as I want to think these are just one-offs or unrelated incidents, they're not. They all stem from the underlying belief in Japan that you're either Japanese or not. Micro actions that are giving shape to a changing political climate.

<h3>7. The political climate towards foreigners in general</h3>

To anyone who has been following Japanese news even slightly I'm sure you might have heard of the Sanseito party or perhaps the "foreigner issue" in Japan. In Japanese they call it "外国人問題", implying foreigners are a problem that must be dealt with. The Sanseito part is a new right-wing political party in Japan that has been gaining a lot of traction and popularity in Japan thanks to being very local about the problems that foreigners are causing. So much so that it caused the current ruling part of Japan, the LDP, (which you might have seen on the news; the prime minister? yea that's her party) to even start cracking down.

The LDP has been in power for decades and has been pretty neutral on foreign topics. Not much has changed since the LDP has been in power many many years ago. But a mini revolution started when this Sanseito party finally started offering change and dealing will long term issues the LDP had been ignoring. This ignited a fire in the LDP and they choose the Prime Minister Takaichi, who is for a very long time, actually getting stuff done within the LDP party; including dealing with foreigners.

Now, I actually agree with 90% of what the LDP is trying to do, however, the sentiment from parties like Sanseito and the overall messaging of "foreigners are problem" is what I have an issue with. By all means, please indeed deport all the illegal immigrants in this country and people not paying their fair share. Unfortunately, all foreigners are getting lumped in with this messaging, including the good ones. So when messaging intended for illegals like "we don't need foreigners" gets out, it gets spun into a messaging that is more or less xenophobic. 

Seeing this daily in the news, from politicians and on TV has been kind of sad, obviously as a foreigner living in Japan. It's like a reverse ICE kind of, except I'm not even illegal. People being against your existence simply because you aren't native Japanese will never not be sad. And its for these reasons as well that I probably won't be changing my citizenship to Japanese. When I first came to Japan, I was eager and excited to change my citizenship because I loved Japan (and still do), but with them raising the requirement from 5 to 10 years, and the fact that no matter how well I speak Japanese, understand the culture and customs, I will always be judged first based on what I look like is quite disappointing and sad.

I brought up these points today to not try and talk bad about Japan. I love Japan. I love living here and 99% of the people I have interacted with have been great. I mean, I literally met my wife here. This place is home to me. But its <i>because </i> its home to me that I feel the need to speak out about my experience living in Japan so far and hopefully have it change for the better. I think alot of Japanese people might not understand how the way they talk or interact with foreigners might actually come off as discriminatory but I wanted to take the time to share this so more people could understand the perspective of us foreigners, told from someone who speaks Japanese and lives here.
